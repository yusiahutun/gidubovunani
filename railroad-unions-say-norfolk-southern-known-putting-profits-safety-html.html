Railroad unions say Norfolk Southern is known for putting profits before safety

The safety record of the railroad behind the toxic train crash in East Palestine, Ohio, is now being probed following the deaths of three employees in separate incidents in just 15 months.

And now union bosses are accusing Norfolk Southern of putting profits before the welfare of rail workers and the general public, with one saying the company actually 'hates' its employees.

'The people who I knew worked over there, their euphemism for Norfolk Southern was the Nazi Southern,' Fritz Edler, the safety representative with Railroad Workers United, an inter-union alliance of rail workers, told DailyMail.com.

Norfolk Southern, headed by CEO Alan Shaw, who rakes in $4.5 million a year, is facing a federal probe over the company's safety practices and culture after the railway saw four derailments in five months 

The February 3 derailment that effectively released tons of toxic waste into the environment in East Palestine, Ohio drew attention to Norfolk's safety record 

'Their notion wasn't just a company that had no high standards, this was a company that essentially hated their employees.'

The National Transportation Safety Board (NTSB) announced on Tuesday that it was opening the investigation into Atlanta-based Norfolk Southern's 'safety practices and culture' following the February 3 derailment in East Palestine.

But the board is expanding the probe to look at other incidents including another derailment in Springfield, Ohio, where a two-and-a-half mile long train went off the tracks on Saturday, and the death of the employees, the most recent of whom died in Cleveland on Tuesday.

 Train conductor Louis Shuster, 46, was killed when a dump truck carrying limestone crashed into his train at a crossing, according to Cleveland police.

Two days later there was another accident, this time near White Plains, Alabama. Thirty cars derailed but this time there were no injuries or toxic leak.

A DailyMail.com investigation into Norfolk Southern – whose network runs fromfrom southern Maine to east Texas – has shown that the company has long faced concerns about its corporate culture.

But that is just the tip of the iceberg at a firm which slashed thousands of staff despite earning record profits in 2022 and paying its boss Alan Shaw $4.5million a year.

Within the last three years Norfolk Southern has fired all five engineers who inspected the heat sensors in the East Palestine area, sensors which are now a key part of the investigation.

Norfolk Southern's practice of putting the heavier load at the rear of their freight trains makes accidents more likely but saves the company money, unions claimed.

Yet despite all this, Norfolk Southern has blown more than $123million on lobbying and paid its shareholders nearly $18billion through stock buybacks and dividends over the past five years. 

Just one month after the East Palestine disaster, another Norfolk Southern train derailed 200 miles southwest, in Springfield, where officials are still cleaning truckloads of toxic waste

The Springfield derailment was the second involving a Norfolk train in barely a month, drawing attention to its safety practices

The derailment in Springfield, Ohio, led to residents being warned to shelter in place

The most recent derailment of a Norfolk Southern train occurred in Alabama in an unpopulated wooded area roughly 70 miles east of Birmingham on Thursday

An overheated bearing is now the focus of the investigation by the NTSB into the East Palestine crash which led to toxic train cars being detonated and a plume of black smoke which has killed fish and left residents in fear for their health.

Shaw, who has a 20-property empire spread across Georgia and Virginia, gave evidence before Congress on Thursday about the accident which led to Norfolk Southern conducting a 'controlled burn' of two train cars carrying the toxic chemical vinyl chloride.

According to a lawsuit filed by residents this released a chemical which was banned after World War I and left them with headaches, coughing and burning feelings on their skin.

Union representatives told DailyMail.com that corporate greed was behind Norfolk Southern skirting safety measures.

They said that the train involved in the derailment, officially called NS 32N, was so prone to issues it was dubbed '32 Nasty' by workers.

In an open letter to Ohio governor Mike DeWine, Jonathan Long, chairman of the BMWED American Rail System Federation, another union representing track workers, claimed that Norfolk Southern's 'cost cutting business model is dangerous to America'.

He complained about the 'level of disregard that Norfolk Southern has for the safety of the railroad's Workers, its track structure, and East Palestine and other American communities where (it) operates'.

Four Norfolk Southern derailments in Ohio in five months 

October 8 - A train derails  in Sandusky, Ohio, leaking paraffin wax which later solidified

November 7 - Train derails in Steubenville, Ohio, dumping garbage into the Ohio river

February 3 -  A huge 38-car derailment in East Palestine leads to the controlled explosion of toxic chemicals

March 4 - A 22-car train derails near Springfield, Ohio, but officials rule out the presence of hazardous materials

Long called Norfolk Southern 'immoral' and said that its business model 'disregards the sanctity of human life for the sake of more record profits'.

Train 32N was made up of 150 cars en route from Madison, Illinois, to Conway, Pennsylvania, when the accident occurred.

A total of 38 cars derailed and began a fire that damaged another 12 cars.

According to the NTSB, railroad bearings can overheat when they have water damage, mechanical damage, are loose or have a defect.

In this case it may have been the combination of a hot axle and plastic pellets which started a fire that appears to have caused the bearings to become too hot and fail.

The NTSB said it was investigating Norfolk Southern in depth because of the 'number and significance' of accidents involving the company's trains since 2021.

The agency is investigating the death of track worker Kolton Helbert, 27, in December 2021 in Reed, Pennsylvania, who was hit by a Norfolk Southern machine as it reversed.

In December last year a Norfolk Southern trainee conductor Walter Griffin, 43, was killed and another injured in an accident in Bessemer, Alabama, when a metal beam protruding from one Norfolk Southern train crashed through his cab as the two trains passed.

Norfolk Southern has a terrible record when it comes to accidents involving hazmat trains as well.

A previous analysis by DailyMail.com found that 654 of the company's hazardous material-carrying cars were involved in accidents in 2022.

That is a six-fold increase on the figures from 2012 where there were just 105, according to Federal Railroad Administration figures.

By contrast, BNSF, the largest railroad in the country, saw a reduction in hazmat cars damaged between 2012 and 2022, from 180 down to 136.

Norfolk Southern Corporation President and CEO, Alan Shaw, testified before a US Senate Committee on Environment and Public Works hearing on the environmental and public health on Thursday

In an open letter to Ohio governor Mike DeWine, Jonathan Long, chairman of the BMWED American Rail System Federation, claimed that Norfolk Southern's 'cost cutting business model is dangerous to America'

Union Pacific Railroad Company, the second largest, had damage to 201 hazmat cars in 2012, compared to 157 in 2022

One of the factors the NTSB will be investigating is why the alarms didn't alert the train crew earlier that there was a problem.

Along the stretch of track where the East Palestine derailment happened, Norfolk Southern has installed hot bearing detectors, or HBDs, which showed a spike in the temperature of the bearing which is suspected as the cause of the crash.

NS 32N passed through three HBD systems before derailing: the first showed the bearing on the 23rd car was 38f above ambient temperature.

The second showed it was 103f above ambient temperature and the final one was 253f, at which point the crew got an alarm and stopped the train but it was too late to stop the carriages coming off the rails.

While the NTSB initial report did not mention any faulty HBDs, NTSB chair Jennifer Homendy has said that if there were another HBD before East Palestine the derailment 'may not have occurred'.

She has called the accident '100 percent preventable'.

Moreover, the Pittsburgh Post-Gazette has reported that footage from a CCTV camera showed one of the carriages on the train that crashed was on fire 20 miles before the derailment, raising questions about whether an HBD wasn't working.

Train workers union the Brotherhood of Railroad Signalmen said that specialized signalmen called 'electronic leaders' were used to maintain the HBDs in the Eastern Region North Division B, which covers Ohio.

To workers, Norfolk 'wasn't just a company that had no high standards' but one that 'essentially hated their employees,' according to Railroad Workers United rep, Fritz Edler

The five electronic leader roles in the area were eliminated within the past three years meaning that fewer experienced signal maintainers would have to service the HBDs, they claim.

But they have inadequate training and do not have time for measures like cleaning, according to Chris Hand, the union's director of research.

Another factor which may have contributed to the crash was the weight distribution of the train – in the case of the East Palestine crash, 40 percent of the weight was grouped in the rear third, said Elder..

'The weight is put at the back because it is quicker and easier to load the train up and saves the train company money.

'NS 32N was made up of numerous trains together. If you can tack it onto the back end of something that's much quicker than if you have to pull the old cars off and put them back.

'It's a momentum thing – imagine putting a golf ball in a sock.

'If you swing it around the weight of the golf ball at the back will make it powerfully go forward when you stop your hand.

'If you're going to do a much heavier train the best way is to put the weight towards the front'.

In East Palestine, the rail carriages 'cascaded' over one another because they had the momentum from behind pushing them, Elder said.

But a spokesperson for the company said the company is careful to distribute weight. 'We employ several safety measures to ensure our trains are balance and operating effectively,' she said. 

Norfolk Southern's decision to fire 3,500 staff during the pandemic has come back to haunt it as demand ramped up over the past 12 months.

The derailment caused an environmental crisis for the city of East Palestine where residents were forced to flee to safety after they were engulfed by a toxic cloud of chemicals 

Petroleum-based chemicals could be seen floating on the top of the water in Leslie Run creek after being contaminated by the toxic spill in East Palestine 

People work as they clean up the site following the derailment of a train carrying hazardous waste in East Palestine, Ohio

The layoffs enabled it to earn a record $3.2billion profit in the final quarter of 2022 but last December Shaw admitted that the firm still hadn't managed to fill all of its posts, some of which involve years of training.

He said: 'Frankly, we still have about 25 percent of our crew locations that are understaffed'.

Other long-standing issues have been battles between the company and workers over Precision Scheduled Railroading, a more efficient way of directing rail traffic.

Rail unions claim that this is contributing to freight companies planning to soon run trains that are miles long with just one operator.

Currently the law requires two conductors but unions claim that is being chipped away at.

In 2021 two unions sued Norfolk Southern for allegedly making train engineers do the jobs of other workers.

In a joint statement the unions said that the shortage of conductors and brakemen is 'so severe' that engineers were being 'ordered' to carry out their duties.

Norfolk Southern's eye on the bottom line contrasts to its largesse when it comes to spending on its corporate interests.

The company has spent over $123million on lobbying since government records began in 1999, including $15 million since 2017, according to an analysis of federal filings.

Norfolk Southern and its 'Good Government Fund' which pools donations by staffers, spent $5,294,025 in political contributions from 2017 through 2021, per disclosures listed on its website.

A further $500,000 was authorized for 2022.

Residents have resorted to wearing oxygen masks to protect themselves after the disaster sparked environmental and health concerns 

A mannequin wearing a gas mask was seen on display at a home in East Palestine during Transportation Secretary Pete Buttigieg's visit to the site of the derailment on February 23

It also made $3,960,014 of contributions to trade organizations, chambers of commerce and tax-exempt organizations over the same period.

The data shows contributions of at least $1,000 each to more than 1,000 candidates, committees and political groups.

Ohio politicians received $182,125 from 2017 through 2021; Pennsylvania politicians received $238,750.

Norfolk Southern has given a total of $298,000 to 16 of the 27 members of the Senate transport committee, including $35,500 to West Virginia Senator Shelley Moore Capito and South Dakota Senator John Thune.

But it wasn't just Republicans who received the company's largesse. Norfolk Southern made a total of 3,563 different donations to politicians of all stripes between 2017 and 2021.

Democratic grandee Rep. Jim Clyburn and his PAC got $50,000 while Senator Tim Kaine, another Democrat, got $30,000 from the Norfolk Southern Good Government Fund between 2017 and 2021.

And the lobbying paid off. The company led attempts to weaken Obama-era policies which improved braking systems on massive trains.

The requirements for trains carrying hazardous flammable materials to have electronically controlled pneumatic brakes were rescinded by the Trump administration as part of its effort to roll back regulations.

While this did not apply to the East Palestine train – it was not a high-hazard flammable train so not covered by the rescinded rule – it showed the power of the rail industry lobbying operation.

Executives say Norfolk Southern has given $3.8 million in aid 'directly to the citizens who are impacted by the incident' – but that is less than Shaw's pay packet for 2021.

In a statement Norfolk Southern said that it is 'committed to the safety and integrity of our defect detectors'.

The company said it was making 'good progress' in inspecting all of the nearly 1,000 wayside detectors on its system.

Norfolk Southern said the wayside detectors on its network 'trigger an alarm at a temperature threshold that is among the lowest in the rail industry'.

The firm said that it 'values all of its employees' and was making 'substantial progress' in recruiting new staff.

A spokeswoman disputed the claim of tense relations with workers and said that is 'not how we would characterize the relationship'.
